A project by Somesh Kolluru

Hi. I'm Somesh. In this project, I use SQL & Power BI to answer business questions and derive key insights to build marketing strategies.

About the project

    This project explores sales data for an automobile manufacturer, using SQL and Power BI to uncover insights.
    The analysis answers key business questions, highlights trends and provides actionable recommendations.

    The given dataset contains eight tables:
    Customers: customer data
    Employees: all employee information
    Offices: sales office information
    Orders: customers' sales orders
    OrderDetails: sales order line for each sales order
    Payments: customers' payment records
    Products: a list of scale model cars
    ProductLines: a list of product line categories
    Here's the Entity Relationship Diagram:
    The business questions posed by the higher management are:
    Q1: Which products should we order more of or less of?
    Q2: How should we match marketing and communication strategies to customer behaviour?
    Q3: How much can we spend on acquiring new customers?

    To answer the above questions, I created SQL queries and Power BI visuals using the following steps:

    SQL
    For Q1
    Calculated the products that were almost out of stock.
    Calculated the products that sold the most.
    Combined the two and listed the products that were the most profitable.
    For Q2
    Calculated the total profits brought by each customer.
    Listed the top 5 most and least valuable customers.
    For Q3
    Calculated the number of new customers acquired per month and decided to spend money on acquisition.
    Calculated the average customer lifetime value.
    Power BI
    I converted the dataset to .csv, uploaded it to PBI Desktop, and cleaned up the data.
    For Q1
    Merged the 'orders' and the 'products' tables and kept all the relevant columns.
    Created measures for 'Low Stock Ratio' and 'Product Performance' using the formulae.
    Used two Stacked Bar Charts in the Report View and added the respective measure to generate the visual.
    For Q2
    Created measures for 'Total Orders' and 'Total Profit' in the 'order details' table.
    Selected a Scatter Chart and added 'Total Orders' to X-axis and 'Total Profit' to Y-axis.
    Also added fields like 'country', 'customernumber' and 'no. of vehicles ordered' for a more detailed visual.
    For Q3
    Merged the necessary tables and created measures for 'CLV' and 'Average CLV per Customer'.
    Created CLV bins to categorise customers based on their lifetime value:
      CLV < 20000 - Low CLV
      CLV >= 20000 and < 50000 - Medium CLV
      CLV >= 50000 - High CLV
    Selected a Histogram (Clustered Column Chart) for the CLV bins.
    Selected a Treemap for CLV distribution by each customer and colour-coded them by 'country'.
    Selected three Cards for 'Total CLV', 'Total Customers' and 'Average CLV per Customer'.

Insights

    Q1: Which products should we order more of or less of?
    The low stock ratio and product performance tell us the most in-demand products.
    Vintage cars and motorcycles are the priority for restocking.
    They sell frequently and they are the highest-performance products.
    This way, we can prioritise stocking and build strategies around these products.
    Q2: How should we match marketing and communication strategies to customer behaviour?
    By calculating customer profit, we have the most valuable and least-committed customers.
    We can determine how to drive loyalty and attract more customers.
    Q3: How much can we spend on acquiring new customers?
    Customer Lifetime Value tells us how much profit an average customer generates during their lifetime with our store.
    We can use it to predict our future profit.
    So, if we get ten new customers next month, we'll earn 390,395 dollars.
    Based on this prediction, we can decide how much we can spend on acquiring new customers.
